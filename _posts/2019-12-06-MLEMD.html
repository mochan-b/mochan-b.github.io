Introduction

Let us first recap a few definitions from probability that we will use to build up to the definitions of MLE/MAP. Since discrete and continuous definitions are different, we have to give slightly different definitions for each of the cases.

We will define probability space which leads to random variables and distribution. Condition probability and Bayes theorem gives us the ability to define the posterior distribution. The posterior distribution then gives us all the tools to explore MLE/MAP.

I have not made the effort to make the definitions mathematically super-accurate or follow a known set of definitions. These are just here to give a rough idea of what the terms mean and to get us to MLE/MAP that does not require a lot of hand-wavey explanations.

Probability

Probability in the common understanding takes a set of possible outcomes and gives a number between 0 and 1 of how likely it is to occur. A fair coin toss has probability of head as 12\frac{1}{2}21​ and probability of tail as 12\frac{1}{2}21​. A fair dice has a probability of each of the faces coming up is 16\frac{1}{6}61​ and the probability of being at least a 3 is 12\frac{1}{2}21​.

Inference

Estimation is the problem where we have data and need to find the distribution of the data. MAP/MLE are different methods for doing this. Estimation is possible after we have the likelihood but before that we review conditional probability and Bayes theorem.

Bayes Theorem

This leads to Bayes theorem,

Likelihood

Given a set of observations, we want to estimate the distribution that best fits the data.

Instead of considering all possible distributions, we will consider a simpler problem of only looking at a set of a family of distributions. For example, for the length of a task, we can consider it to come from a normal distribution. For a neural network, we can fix the architecture and connections and only worry about the weights.

By considering a family of distributions, the distribution can be represented a random variable of the parameters of the distribution. For example, for a normal distribution, we consider the random variable of normal variable parameters of mean and standard deviation. Let Φ\PhiΦ be the set of the family of distributions and our random variable is Θ:Φ→R2\Theta : \Phi \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2Θ:Φ→R2. For nueral networks, if there are ddd weights in the neural network, Θ:Φ→Rd\Theta : \Phi \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^dΘ:Φ→Rd.

Suppose we are using nnn observations for inference. Let Ω\OmegaΩ be the set of observations and thus, our observations come from Ωn\Omega^nΩn. Let XXX be our random variable and x=(x1,x2,…,xn)\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)x=(x1​,x2​,…,xn​) be our set of observations. Using the notation of Θ\ThetaΘ as the random variable to describe the parameters for our distribution, we want to find the function that gives us the parameter of the distribution θ\thetaθ from the data. The function would be something like LΘ∣X(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}_{\Theta|X}(\theta|x)LΘ∣X​(θ∣x) which gives us the probability of distribution parameter from the data.

However, the function LΘ∣X(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}_{\Theta|X}(\theta|x)LΘ∣X​(θ∣x) does not make sense as a conditional probability for random variables because we required the domain of Θ\ThetaΘ and XXX to be the same in our definition. To fix this, we will consider the domain Ωn×Φ\Omega^n \times \PhiΩn×Φ and our random variables will be X~:Ωn×Φ→Rn\tilde{X} : \Omega^n \times \Phi \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^nX~:Ωn×Φ→Rn and Θ~:Ωn×Φ→Rd\tilde{\Theta} : \Omega^n \times \Phi \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^dΘ~:Ωn×Φ→Rd. Then, given that our observation probability space is (Ωn,F,P)(\Omega^n, \mathcal{F}, P)(Ωn,F,P), we define L(X~=x×θ)=P(X=x)\mathcal{L}(\tilde{X} = \mathbf{x} \times \theta) = P(X =\mathbf{x})L(X~=x×θ)=P(X=x). If the probability space for the distributions is (Φ,G,Q)(\Phi, \mathcal{G}, Q)(Φ,G,Q) then, we define L(Θ~=x×θ)=Q(Θ=θ)\mathcal{L}(\tilde{\Theta} = \mathbf{x} \times \theta) = Q(\Theta =\theta)L(Θ~=x×θ)=Q(Θ=θ). Thus, we can define the function as the likelihood function and,
LΘ~∣X~(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}_{\tilde{\Theta}|\tilde{X}}(\theta|x)LΘ~∣X~​(θ∣x)

The likelihood function gives the probability that our given set of statistical observations x\mathbf{x}x came from a set of statistical parameters θ\thetaθ.

By using Bayes theorem for random variables and using fff for the joint probability distribution and discarding the tilde in our random variable names,
LΘ∣X(θ∣x)=fX∣Θ(x∣θ)fΘ(θ)fX(x)\mathcal{L}_{\Theta|X} (\theta | x) = f_{X|\Theta}(\mathbf{x} | \theta) \frac{f_\Theta(\theta)}{f_X(\mathbf{x})}LΘ∣X​(θ∣x)=fX∣Θ​(x∣θ)fX​(x)fΘ​(θ)​

fΘ(θ)f_\Theta(\theta)fΘ​(θ) is called the prior distribution and is the distribution of our parameters. If we have no idea what it could be, this is just uniform distribution and a constant value. In cases where we have a prior distribution of Θ\ThetaΘ, it is not constant.

The term fX(x)=∫Φf(x∣v)g(v)dvf_X(\mathbf{x}) = \int_\Phi f(x|v) g(v) dvfX​(x)=∫Φ​f(x∣v)g(v)dv where ggg is the distribution of Θ\ThetaΘ. By integrating on all terms in Θ\ThetaΘ, this term does not depend on θ\thetaθ and since our observation data is fixed, we can think of it as being constant.

Note that fX∣θ(x∣θ)f_{X|\theta}(\mathbf{x}|\theta)fX∣θ​(x∣θ) is the probability of the random variable XXX given the distribution has parameters θ\thetaθ. The domain of XXX is all nnn of our observations. It is not one observation but all of them. In inference, we would use get a single observation xxx and we would return P(x∣θ)P(x|\theta)P(x∣θ). Here fff is a different function that takes nnn observations x\mathbf{x}x and provides a probability for it.

When θ\thetaθ is the probability of the coin toss landing head, then XXX is the number of heads observed and fff is the Bernoulli distribution of xxx heads out of nnn tosses.

When θ\thetaθ are the parameters of the normal distribution, fff is simply the product of nnn normal distributions

f(x∣θ=μ,σ2)=∏i=1nf(xi∣μ,σ2)f(\mathbf{x} | \theta = \mu, \sigma^2) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i | \mu, \sigma^2)f(x∣θ=μ,σ2)=i=1∏n​f(xi​∣μ,σ2)

In deep learning, x\mathbf{x}x is all our training samples. In this case, fff is the cross-entropy which we will discuss below. The basic idea is that the training samples produce a distribution whereas the output of the neural network using θ\thetaθ as the parameters produces another distribution. Cross-entropy measures the difference between the two distributions.

Estimation Using Likelihood

The distribution L(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}(\theta|\mathbf{x})L(θ∣x) gives us all the information to do inference. Note that we are omitting the random variable terms and the above term should be LΘ~∣X~(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}_{\tilde{\Theta}|\tilde{X}}(\theta|x)LΘ~∣X~​(θ∣x). Also note that we have already selected the family of distributions and θ\thetaθ is the parameter that gives the exact distribution out of the family of distributions.

From Bayes theorem and our argument that fX(x)f_X(\mathbf{x})fX​(x) is a constant and let c=1fX(x)c=\frac{1}{f_X(\mathbf{x})}c=fX​(x)1​, we have that

Here again we are dropping all of the random variables for cleaner notation.

Since L(θ∣x)\mathcal{L}(\theta|x)L(θ∣x) is a distribution, we want a single parameter θ\thetaθ. We can take a statistical property like mean or mode of the distribution to select a value θ⋆\theta^\starθ⋆ as our estimate.

  • Mode: arg max⁡θL(θ∣x)\argmax_{\theta} \mathcal{L} (\theta|\mathbf{x})argmaxθ​L(θ∣x) - MLE or MAP
  • Mean : Eθ[L(θ∣x)]\mathbb{E}_\theta \left[ \mathcal{L} (\theta|\mathbf{x}) \right]Eθ​[L(θ∣x)]

If we have no knowledge of θ\thetaθ, then f(θ)f(\theta)f(θ) is a uniform distribution and is constant. In this case denoting c1=c  f(θ)c_1=c \; f(\theta)c1​=cf(θ),
L(θ∣x)=c1  f(x∣θ)\mathcal{L} (\theta | x) = c_1 \; f(\mathbf{x} | \theta)L(θ∣x)=c1​f(x∣θ)

If f(θ)f(\theta)f(θ) is not constant, we call it a prior distribution. This means that we have some guess or information about the distribution of θ\thetaθ. The likelihood will be an improvement over the current prior given the dataset x\mathbf{x}x.

Using the arg max without a prior (or uniform prior) is called MLE estimation. Using a prior distribution, we call it an MAP estimate.

MLE Estimation

Given the likelihood function L(X∣θ)\mathcal{L}(X|\theta)L(X∣θ), then the MLE for θ\thetaθ is given by
θMLE=arg max⁡θL(x∣θ)\theta_{MLE} = \argmax_\theta \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}|\theta)θMLE​=θargmax​L(x∣θ)

For computational reasons, we can also define the function in log⁡\loglog.
θMLE=arg max⁡θ[log⁡L(X∣θ)]\theta_{MLE} = \argmax_\theta \left[ \log \mathcal{L}(X|\theta) \right]θMLE​=θargmax​[logL(X∣θ)]
And, thus, we will be maximizing in log likelikhood.

Conclusion

This article gives a quick introduction to MLE and MAP and how they are derived from the Bayes theorem for random variables. These methods are general methods of estimation and using them still involves finding the right likelihood function for the problem that can give the best estimate of its parameters.
