Policy Gradient Theorem

Definitions

Markov Decision Process (1)

A Markov decision process MMM is a tuple where
M=(S,A,Ra)M = (S, A, R_a)M=(S,A,Ra​)
and

  • SSS is the finite set of states
  • AAA is the finite set of actions_ and AsA_sAs​ is the set of actions for state s∈Ss \in Ss∈S
  • Ra(s)R_a(s)Ra​(s) is the reward from state sss taking action aaa

Suppose that MMM is a one-step MDP. The episode starts at state s∈Ss \in Ss∈S. The episode ends after one step and with reward rrr.

Policy

Given a state sss, the policy π(s,a)∈[0,1]\pi(s,a) \in [0,1]π(s,a)∈[0,1] gives us the probability that we will take action aaa from state sss.

Let θ∈Rn\theta \in \mathbb{R}^nθ∈Rn be some set of parameters that determine our policy. We denote such a policy by πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​.

Theorem

Let rrr be the random variable of the reward we get from taking an action. Let J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ) be the expected reward from following policy πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ with parameters θ\thetaθ.
J(θ)=Eπθ[r]J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} [r]J(θ)=Eπθ​​[r]

Suppose we start at state sss. Then,
∇θJ(θ)=Eπθ[(∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a))r]\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} \left[ \left( \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a) \right) r \right]∇θ​J(θ)=Eπθ​​[(∇θ​logπθ​(s,a))r]

  • The notation Eπθ[r]\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} [r]Eπθ​​[r] is different than the standard method of writing expectations since we have a subscript πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ on the expectation. Usually, we have a random variable XXX and then E[X]=∑i=1kxipi\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^k x_i p_iE[X]=∑i=1k​xi​pi​. In our case, rrr denotes the random variable of the reward for each action taken.   • The notation Eπθ[(∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a))r]\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} \left[ \left( \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a) \right) r \right]Eπθ​​[(∇θ​logπθ​(s,a))r] has inside it term (∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a)\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a)∇θ​logπθ​(s,a)) that are not random variables. The ∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a)\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a)∇θ​logπθ​(s,a) in this context should be interpreted as the random variable with given state sss and over all possible actions aaa. Rock Paper Scissors MDP

As an example of one step MDP, we will use rock, paper and scissors game. We play the game for one step and we either win, lose or draw.

For the first example of the game we will assume

  • we know what the opponent is going to play.
  • opponent will play rock (since we specify a starting state in our theorem - we will generalize it to any starting state below)

States

We have 3 states for what we know the opponent will play.

  • srs_{r}sr​: opponent plays rock
  • sps_{p}sp​: opponent plays paper
  • sss_{s}ss​: opponent plays scissors

Actions

We either rock, paper or scissors.

  • ara_rar​ : play rock
  • apa_pap​ : play paper
  • asa_sas​ : play scissors

Rewards

We get +1 points if we win the game, 0 if the we draw the game and -1 if we lose the game.

Policy

Let θ∈R\theta \in \mathbb{R}θ∈R be the parameter for our policy. We define πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ as the following:
πθ(sr,ap)=σ(θ)πθ(sr,ar)=1−σ(θ)2πθ(sr,as)=1−σ(θ)2\begin{aligned} \pi_\theta(s_r, a_p) &= \sigma(\theta) \\ \pi_\theta(s_r, a_r) &= \frac{1 - \sigma(\theta)}{2} \\ \pi_\theta(s_r, a_s) &= \frac{1 - \sigma(\theta)}{2} \\ \end{aligned}πθ​(sr​,ap​)πθ​(sr​,ar​)πθ​(sr​,as​)​=σ(θ)=21−σ(θ)​=21−σ(θ)​​
where σ(x)\sigma(x)σ(x) is the sigmoid function given by
σ(x)=11+e−x\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}σ(x)=1+e−x1​

The sigmoid function maps (−∞,∞)(-\infty, \infty)(−∞,∞) to (0,1)(0, 1)(0,1). Then, the above policy is a valid probability as the sum over all actions sums up to 1.

When θ→−∞\theta \rightarrow -\inftyθ→−∞, πθ(sr,ap)→0\pi_\theta(s_r, a_p) \rightarrow 0πθ​(sr​,ap​)→0, and θ→∞\theta \rightarrow \inftyθ→∞, πθ(sr,ap)→1\pi_\theta(s_r, a_p) \rightarrow 1πθ​(sr​,ap​)→1. Thus, the smaller the value of θ\thetaθ, the smaller the probability of us choose the action apa_pap​ for paper.

Policy Gradient

Then, we have reward 1 for playing paper and -1 for playing scissors,
∇θJ(θ)=∑a∈A∇θπθ(s,a)  Ra(s)=1⋅∇θπθ(sr,ap)+(−1)⋅∇θπθ(sr,as)=σ′(θ)+σ′(θ)2=32σ′(θ)\begin{aligned} \nabla_\theta J(\theta) &= \sum_{a \in A} \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(s,a) \; R_a(s) \\ &= 1 \cdot \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(s_r, a_p) + (-1) \cdot \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(s_r, a_s) \\ &= \sigma^\prime(\theta) + \frac{\sigma^\prime(\theta)}{2} \\ &= \frac{3}{2} \sigma^\prime(\theta) \end{aligned}∇θ​J(θ)​=a∈A∑​∇θ​πθ​(s,a)Ra​(s)=1⋅∇θ​πθ​(sr​,ap​)+(−1)⋅∇θ​πθ​(sr​,as​)=σ′(θ)+2σ′(θ)​=23​σ′(θ)​

We do the parameter update using the rule
θ=θ+α∇θJ(θ)\theta = \theta + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta)θ=θ+α∇θ​J(θ)
where α∈R\alpha \in \mathbb{R}α∈R is the learning rate.

Since σ′>0\sigma^\prime > 0σ′>0, θ\thetaθ is always increasing. Thus, πθ(sr,ap)\pi_\theta(s_r, a_p)πθ​(sr​,ap​) goes towards 1 and the probability for other actions towards 0. Thus, the policy gradient will choose to play paper for the rock that the opponent plays.

Generalization (1) - With Transition Probability

We can generalize the MDP to have a transition probability.

In the first definition of MDP, if we are in state sss and we take action aaa, we would transition to a state s′s^\primes′ and obtain single reward for the action.

Here the generalization results is that when taking an action aaa, it now gives us a probability of transitioning to each of the states in SSS. Before an action from state sss would only take you another specific state s′s^\primes′. Now, there is a probability for transition to every state of SSS. If the probability of transitioning to state s′s^\primes′ is 1 and others 0, it is effectively the same as without the generalization.

For reward, if we are in state sss and we transition to state s′s^\primes′, the reward is given by Ra(s,s′)R_a(s, s^\prime)Ra​(s,s′).

A Markov decision process (2) MMM is a tuple where
M=(S,A,Pa,Ra)M = (S, A, P_a, R_a)M=(S,A,Pa​,Ra​)
be

  • SSS is the finite set of states
  • AAA is the finite set of actions_ and AsA_sAs​ is the set of actions for state s∈Ss \in Ss∈S
  • Pa(s,s′)P_a(s, s^\prime)Pa​(s,s′) is the transition probability from state sss to another state s′s^\primes′ by taking action aaa
  • Ra(s,s′)R_a(s, s^\prime)Ra​(s,s′) is the reward from state sss to s′s^\primes′

Policy Gradient Theorem

Let MMM me an MDP(2) as defined above. Let rrr be the random variable of the reward we get from taking an action. Let J(θ)J(\theta)J(θ) be the expected reward from following policy πθ\pi_\thetaπθ​ with parameters θ\thetaθ.
J(θ)=Eπθ[r]J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} [r]J(θ)=Eπθ​​[r]

Suppose we start at state sss. Then,
∇θJ(θ)=Eπθ[(∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a))r]\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} \left[ \left( \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a) \right) r \right]∇θ​J(θ)=Eπθ​​[(∇θ​logπθ​(s,a))r]

Proof

The definition of MDP has changed and thus, when we take an action aaa, we have a transition probability of going from state sss to state s′s^\primes′ with probability Pa(s,s′)P_a(s, s^\prime)Pa​(s,s′).

Thus, we have to sum over all subsequent states that we might go to after an action is taken.
J(θ)=Eπθ[r]=∑a∈A∑s′∈Sπθ(s,a)Pa(s,s′)Ra(s,s′)\begin{aligned} J(\theta) &= \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} [r] \\ &= \sum_{a \in A} \sum_{s^\prime \in S} \pi_\theta(s, a) P_a(s, s^\prime) R_a(s, s^\prime) \end{aligned}J(θ)​=Eπθ​​[r]=a∈A∑​s′∈S∑​πθ​(s,a)Pa​(s,s′)Ra​(s,s′)​

Now, taking the gradient and noting that the transition probability and reward does not depend on θ\thetaθ, and so we have,
∇θJ(θ)=∇θ(∑a∈A∑s′∈Sπθ(s,a)Pa(s,s′)Ra(s,s′))=∑a∈A∑s′∈S∇θπθ(s,a)Pa(s,s′)Ra(s,s′)=∑a∈A∑s′∈S[∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a)]πθ(s,a)Pa(s,s′)Ra(s,s′)=Eπθ[(∇θlog⁡πθ(s,a))r]\begin{aligned} \nabla_\theta J(\theta) &= \nabla_\theta \left( \sum_{a \in A} \sum_{s^\prime \in S} \pi_\theta(s, a) P_a(s, s^\prime) R_a(s, s^\prime) \right)\\ &= \sum_{a \in A} \sum_{s^\prime \in S} \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(s, a) P_a(s, s^\prime) R_a(s, s^\prime) \\ &= \sum_{a \in A} \sum_{s^\prime \in S} \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s, a) \right] \pi_\theta(s, a) P_a(s, s^\prime) R_a(s, s^\prime) \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} \left[ \left( \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(s,a) \right) r \right] \end{aligned}∇θ​J(θ)​=∇θ​(a∈A∑​s′∈S∑​πθ​(s,a)Pa​(s,s′)Ra​(s,s′))=a∈A∑​s′∈S∑​∇θ​πθ​(s,a)Pa​(s,s′)Ra​(s,s′)=a∈A∑​s′∈S∑​[∇θ​logπθ​(s,a)]πθ​(s,a)Pa​(s,s′)Ra​(s,s′)=Eπθ​​[(∇θ​logπθ​(s,a))r]​

Rock Paper Scissors

In the previous example of rock, paper, scissors, we knew what the opponent was going to play. With the new formulation of the MDP, we now divide the state into what we believe the opponent will play and what the opponent plays after we perform our action.

States

We have the 6 states, 3 for before we play and 3 after we play. The 3 before we play will stand for what we think the opponent will play. The 3 after we play will be what the opponent played.
